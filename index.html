Arizona vs. Gant (2009)

Summary

In early 2008 Arizona officers responded to a call regarding a house to have some seemingly suspicious activity. When the officers arrived Rodney Gant answered and responded saying the owner would be home later that day. When the officers ran Gant’s license they saw that Gant had been driving with a suspended license and had an outstanding warrant for his arrest. The officers then put Gant under arrest and placed him in the squad care. After, they search Gant's car finding a handgun and cocaine. During his trial the defendant asked the Arizona Supreme court if they could suppress the evidence found in his car on the account of the fourth amendment. He believed that the officers had unreasonable cause to search his vehicle.
Ultimately, the judge denied the request and he was charged on the account of cocaine possession. The Arizona Supreme court's main reasoning behind it’s decision was that Gant voluntarily left his vehicles, which would then allow the officers to search his car.

Ruling

The Supreme court decided the officer’s search was unwarranted and in violation of the fourth amendment. Their reasoning was that the officers should not have searched his car unless they had a reason related to the arrest. Justice John Paul Stevensy, Justices Antonin G. Scalia, David H. Souter, Clarence Thomas, and Ruth Bader Ginsburg had majority rule stating, “warrantless searches are per se unreasonable” and also that this scenario did not fall under the small exception to this statement, The remaining four: Justice Samuel A. Alito Chief Justice John G. Roberts, Justices Anthony M. Kennedy and Stephen G. Breyer were against this decision saying that the officers decisions weren’t in violation of the defendant’s rights. With a 5 to 4 vote in favor of the defendant Gant was only charged on the account of driving with a suspended license.
